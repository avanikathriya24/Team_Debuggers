Tracking the Growing Threat

In 1981, an estimated 5,000 objects were in orbit, but new detection systems revealed a growing number. By 2019, this count surged to nearly 20,000 large objects.

By 2002, experts predicted that specific LEO regions (900-1,000 km and 1,500 km altitude) had already surpassed critical density, signaling potential collision cascades.

Hugh Lewis's 2009 prediction highlighted a 50% increase in space debris threat over the next decade, with a fourfold increase expected within 50 years.

As of 2019, over 128 million pieces of debris smaller than 1 cm, 900,000 pieces between 1-10 cm, and at least 37,000 pieces larger than 10 cm were estimated to be in orbit.

Weekly, more than 13,000 close calls are monitored by global space surveillance networks, underscoring the persistent risk of collision.

By 2020, there were approximately 8,000 metric tons of debris orbiting Earth, with expectations of this figure continuing to rise.
